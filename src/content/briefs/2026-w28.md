Eighteen months into the crisis, the market has stopped treating the southern
Red Sea as a disruption and started treating it as a feature. Weekly transits
through Bab el-Mandeb held between 26 and 30 for a fourth consecutive month,
roughly a third of the pre-crisis baseline. The ships still passing are a
distinct population: smaller, older, regionally traded, and increasingly
insured outside the main London market.

The rerouting index tells the structural half of the story. Cape of Good Hope
roundings remain near their highs, and the operators who committed to the
longer route have now built schedules, bunker contracts and port rotations
around it. A reopening of the strait would no longer snap the network back in
weeks; the friction is priced in on the way out too.

Two secondary signals deserve attention. First, GPS interference reports
around Hormuz rose again this week, continuing a pattern that has made
jamming the most frequently logged incident category this year. The
navigational effect is limited; the insurance effect is not, since position
integrity underpins both breach-of-warranty clauses and casualty
investigation. Second, listed-area language in war-risk endorsements keeps
widening: underwriters are drawing zones faster than circulars retire them.

What would change our reading: two consecutive weeks of Bab el-Mandeb
transits above 45, a measurable fall in Hormuz interference reports, or a
listed-area retirement that survives a renewal cycle. None of the three is
in sight.
